About

Hello hello! I'm Hannah Toru, though you may also find me published under Hannah Shay. Originally born in mountainous Colorado, I spent most of my life in California. I earned a bachelor's degree in environmental chemistry from UC Davis, before earning a master's in physical chemistry from the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign and a PhD in chemistry from MIT.
I'm a curious and creative, and enjoy conducting experiments to investigate our natural world.

While at the Department of Chemistry at MIT, where I bridged spectroscopy and computational tools to study fundamental chemistry and physical chemistry. I also developed a new statistical tool to analyze and make use of machine learning results for astrochemists.

Previously, I obtained my master's from the University of Illinois at Urbana Champaign, where I used computational chemistry methods and inductively coupled plasma mass spec (ICP-MS) to study isotope chemistry and fractionation, mostly for understanding water contamination but also with applications to paleoredox. Before that, I was at University of California, Davis, where I did a post-bacc project in analytical/physical chemistry, building a new piezoelectric driver component for a new microwave/RF radiation instrument.

During my undergrad at UC Davis, I completely research in several subjects. These included a biosensor/nanolipoprotein aggregation surface analysis study and a small molecule drug discovery synthesis project and method development. I also did a short stint at Osaka University for a study abroad thesis on bioinorganic catalysis.

In my spare time, I enjoy hanging out with my partner and dog, stargazing, hiking and crafts. I also volunteer with several organizations around Boston, such as with the National Park Service, the Department of Conservation and Recreation and the Cambridge Office of Tourism. I also dabble in roller skating, martial arts and photography.

Projects

• Automated Spectral Analysis In astrochemistry, one major challenge is selecting molecules to pursue. Given that telescope dish time, people power and laboratory time is quite limited, it's crucial that the decisions we make are supported by more than individual intuition. This project took aspects of machine learning, radiofrequency instrumentation and knowledge of quantum molecular behavior to statistically assign likelihoods of detection to molecules in a list to be in a certain environment. You can find the resulting journal article here. The repo can also be found here, though it is in progress of being cleaned up.
skills developed: Python programming, machine learning, algorithm development, data analysis

• FleshHelmet: The Helmet that Feels Like Flesh and Lights Up As part of my final project for the infamously rigorous and speedy How To Make (Almost) Anything course at MIT, I created this helmet that I thought would be a fun accessory to wear to concerts. The class is combined with electrical engineering, mechanical engineering and architecture, so as a chemist, I learned lots! The project used over 12 different fabrication techniques, including embedded programming, electronics and PCB board production, networking and communication and computer controlled machining. You can read more about it here. • Piezoelectric driver.
